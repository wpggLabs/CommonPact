# Adoption and Network Bootstrap

An open protocol can be technically correct and socially empty. CommonPact must address adoption without becoming the mandatory platform it opposes.

## Bootstrap principles

1. Begin with an existing bounded community, not a global empty marketplace.
2. Solve interoperability between at least two clients before chasing consumer scale.
3. Let operators charge transparently for real work without owning the protocol.
4. Preserve export, multi-provider choice, and self-hosting paths from the first pilot.
5. Measure practical concentration, not only formal decentralization.

## Who may operate infrastructure

- cooperatives;
- nonprofits and mutual-aid groups;
- municipalities or institutions;
- commercial application providers;
- independent relay or hosting providers;
- communities running shared infrastructure;
- individuals using direct or self-hosted tools.

No category receives exclusive protocol status.

## Sustainable service models

Operators may use subscriptions, cooperative dues, hosting charges, transaction services, support contracts, integrations, insurance or payment referral revenue, grants, sponsorships, or transparent commissions. A fee becomes problematic when protocol access depends on one unavoidable collector.

## Cold-start strategy

A credible first deployment SHOULD have:

- a defined community with repeated interactions;
- an accountable operator or research partner;
- one narrow profile and jurisdiction;
- explicit support and incident ownership;
- compatible clients or export tooling;
- a stop condition if safety, reliability, or participation thresholds fail.

## Spam and discovery quality

Open discovery requires rate limits, expiring intents, local filters, relay policies, proof-of-work or deposits only where proportionate, community attestations, block lists, and abuse reporting. No single anti-spam mechanism is globally mandatory.

## Migration from closed platforms

CommonPact cannot force incumbent platforms to export data. Adoption paths may include:

- new community networks;
- optional import of user-owned records where lawful;
- parallel use with existing services;
- cooperatives or institutions adopting compatible clients;
- public procurement requiring interoperability;
- open-source tools for recurring known-party coordination.

## Recentralization indicators

The project SHOULD treat a provider as practically mandatory when it controls a dominant share of discovery reach, push delivery, identity acceptance, payments, reputation, app distribution, certification, or user-held history and switching becomes materially difficult.

Metrics should include provider concentration, multi-homing rates, export success, client switching success, relay diversity, fee transparency, and dependency on proprietary services.
