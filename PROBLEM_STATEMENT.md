# Problem Statement

## Closed coordination networks

Many platforms combine communication protocol, marketplace, identity, reputation, payment, policy, and operations into one closed product.

Consequences include:

- users cannot reach counterparties through another compatible application;
- providers lose customer access if removed from one platform;
- reputation and history are not portable;
- ranking and fee rules may change unilaterally;
- the platform can charge because it owns the practical route to the network;
- cooperative, municipal, nonprofit, and specialized clients cannot interoperate;
- platform failure can destroy access and records.

## The middleman distinction

The problem is not that every intermediary is useless. Payment processors settle money. Insurers pool risk. Identity providers perform checks. Support teams respond to users. Moderators enforce policy. Relays deliver data. These may provide real value or be legally required.

The problem is **mandatory bundling and ownership**: one platform controls the whole coordination path and forces all parties to use its bundled services.

CommonPact separates shared coordination semantics, profile-specific domain rules, application experience, transport infrastructure, and optional operational or financial services.

## Requirements derived from the problem

A credible alternative requires a stable event model, user-held authorization, privacy-safe discovery, canonical agreement, consistent lifecycle handling under duplicates and reordering, portable evidence, profile-specific safeguards, replaceable transports, clear responsibility boundaries, and independent conformance evidence.

Removing a platform interface without addressing trust, privacy, safety, operations, adoption, and regulation would not solve the underlying problem.
